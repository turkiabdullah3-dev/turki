Equations Reference

Schwarzschild Radius

Exact

Where: r_s = Schwarzschild radius, G = gravitational constant, M = black hole mass, c = speed of light

Explain like I'm 10:

This is the radius of the event horizon - the point of no return. If you get closer than this, even light can't escape!

What you see:

The black circle at the center of the visualization. For a 10 solar mass black hole, this is about 30 km.

Time Dilation Factor

Exact

Where: α(r) = time dilation factor, r = distance from center, r_s = Schwarzschild radius

Explain like I'm 10:

Time runs slower near the black hole! If α = 0.5, your clock ticks at half speed compared to someone far away.

What you see:

The "Time Dilation α" value in the info panel. As you get closer (smaller distance), α gets smaller and time slows down.

Gravitational Redshift

Exact

Where: z = redshift factor, r = distance from center, r_s = Schwarzschild radius

Explain like I'm 10:

Light loses energy climbing out of gravity. Red light is lower energy than blue, so we call it "redshift".

What you see:

The "Redshift z" value. Larger values mean light is more stretched and redder.

Photon Sphere

Exact

Where: r_ph = photon sphere radius, r_s = Schwarzschild radius

Explain like I'm 10:

At this special distance, light can orbit the black hole in a circle! It's like a racetrack for photons.

What you see:

The faint blue ring around the black hole, at 1.5 times the event horizon radius.

Tidal Acceleration

Approximate

Where: a_tidal = tidal force, G = gravitational constant, M = mass, L = object length, r = distance

Explain like I'm 10:

Gravity is stronger on your feet than your head (if falling feet-first). This stretching force is "spaghettification"!

What you see:

The "Tidal Force" value, assuming a 2-meter tall object. Gets huge near the horizon!

Morris-Thorne Metric

Exact

Where: ds² = spacetime interval, Φ(r) = redshift function, b(r) = shape function, r = radial coordinate, dΩ² = angular part

Explain like I'm 10:

This describes the shape of spacetime around a wormhole - like a tunnel connecting two places in the universe.

What you see:

The curved embedding diagram showing how space bends to form a tunnel.

Throat Condition

Exact

Where: b(r₀) = shape function at throat, r₀ = throat radius

Explain like I'm 10:

The throat is the narrowest part of the wormhole tunnel. This equation says the tunnel has a minimum size.

What you see:

The glowing ring at the center - the wormhole's throat.

Flare-Out Condition

Exact

Where: b'(r₀) = derivative of shape function at throat

Explain like I'm 10:

The tunnel must get wider as you move away from the throat - it can't close up on you!

What you see:

How the embedding curves widen away from the center.

Key Terms

Event Horizon

The boundary around a black hole beyond which nothing can escape, not even light.

Time Dilation

The slowing down of time in strong gravitational fields relative to a distant observer.

Redshift

The stretching of light waves to longer (redder) wavelengths due to gravity or motion.

Spacetime

The four-dimensional fabric combining space and time, which can be curved by mass and energy.

Exotic Matter

Hypothetical matter with negative energy density, required to keep wormholes open.

Schwarzschild Solution

The exact solution to Einstein's field equations for a non-rotating, spherically symmetric black hole.

Scientific Honesty

What This Is

An interactive educational visualization using scientifically accurate equations from General Relativity.

What This Is NOT

This is not a full general relativistic ray-tracing simulation. True photon paths around black holes require solving geodesic equations numerically.

Assumptions Made

  • Non-rotating (Schwarzschild) black hole
  • No accretion disk physics (visual approximation only)
  • Observer at rest relative to black hole
  • Static spacetime (no time evolution)
  • Vacuum solution (no surrounding matter except visualization)

Wormhole Note

Traversable wormholes remain purely theoretical. No exotic matter has been discovered, and it may be impossible to create or stabilize such structures.

Numerical Accuracy

All physics calculations use safety clamps to prevent NaN and Infinity values, ensuring stable visualization even at extreme conditions.
